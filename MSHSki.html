Mt St Helens, Washington, Spring Skiing– April 15, 2007

Mt St Helens is one of the prime spring skiing destinations in the Portland area.  It’s close, access is good, it’s a relatively easy approach, the conditions are usually predictable, and the terrain is excellent for backcountry adventures.  The 2006-2007 snow season was the first open season since the volcano was closed to hiking after the 2004 eruptions.  I couldn’t wait to get back after three years of no access.

There’s no denying it: ski climbing is hard and sometimes mind numbing work.  I finally figured out that if you do a good part of your hiking in the dark, the numbing part is hidden from your mind.  I set my alarm for 12:30 am and was out the door by 1:30 am.  I arrived at the trail head at 3 am and found mostly clear skies with snow flurries.  It was a little cold for this time of year and the spring had been mostly rainy.  I knew that even if the skiing wasn’t going to be so good, at least I’d get a good cloud show.

By 7 am I was a good way up the mountain (6000 ft) and finally above the clouds that had been intermittently surrounding me.  Watching clouds swirl just below can be quite hypnotic so I parked myself and had a snack and watched the show.

It must have been the cold weather that was causing the huge steam clouds over the crater.  Here’s one that was about average size.  It cast a sulfur yellow shadow on the snow.

I continued on with my slog, enjoying the sunny morning.  I soon realized that, being the first one on the trail, I’d also have to break trail.  There was new windblown snow that had pretty much covered up any boot holes that would make my climb easier.  It was slow going but I knew I’d have appreciative followers below.  They were gaining fast.  Finally near the top, the group in the picture below overtook me and thanked me for the well spaced steps.  They proceeded to lay sloppy and completely useless steps for me.  I guess the gratitude was only superficial.  They did make for a nice picture subject though.

I rested and watched the hordes arrive.  Soon it would become very crowded with over 100 people at the top when I left.  It was fun to hear the comments as each one arrived at the top.

It's me! Proof I was there.  Mt Adams is in the distance.

The highlight of a visit to the top of Mt St Helens is examining the new lava dome.  It’s continually active and makes the old dome look like an ant hill.  This is about as close as I was willing to get to the cornice edge.

Eventually I decided the time was right to go skiing.  I’ve made enough ski trips down this mountain to know the choice spots and took off in my “secret direction”.  The top was nice and steep but peppered with marble sized ice chunks.  I got past that junk and found some of the nicest most sugary corn I’d ever seen.  The cloudy lower elevations kept the corn near the bottom nice and chilled all the way down back to the car.  I had low expectations for the day but I found it be one of the best spring backcountry days I’ve ever experienced.
