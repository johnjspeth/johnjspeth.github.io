Watching the Elk Rut near Mt Margaret - September 17, 2006

In the five years I lived in Vancouver close to Mt St Helens, I never once visited the Mt Margaret Backcountry.  My goal this day was to climb Mt Margaret but my unexpected treat was watching the annual elk rut.  Now, because of the devastation caused by the 1980 volcano blast and subsequent recovery, the area is home to a huge elk population that finds plenty of space, food and cover. 

After a very long drive following the early morning rubber-necking hunters on Mt St Helens windy roads, I finally started my hike at 7am.  I reached Norway Pass quickly and here’s what I found over the top: the mountain topped with a rainy cloud and some new snow.  That’s Spirit Lake at the foot of the mountain still half filled with logs.

There was still a few more miles of traversing the ridge to get to Mt Margaret.  As I got closer and higher, I heard more and more of this, constant elk bugling, a very eerie sound if you’ve never heard it before.  September was the time of the annual elk rut.  I could hear them but I just couldn’t see them (yet).  I proceeded onward with my final destination coming into view.  Here’s Mt Margaret on the left and some knobby unnamed peak on the right.  They’re both about the same peak elevation but Mt Margaret is a little farther away and much more massive.

My turnaround time was coming up so I decided to climb the unnamed peak, relax for a while, and then start back.  As I climbed the peak, the elk bugling got louder but still no sighting.  I arrived at the top and found a birds-eye view in all directions.  Now I could finally see several elk herds, one herd for each of three separate valleys around me.  The bugling was coming from all directions probably from many lone bulls looking for some action.  Finally, I was treated to a real Marty Stouffer moment: I will soon witness a lone bull elk hijack a herd of cows from a younger bull. 

All the “action” happened right below me.  The first cow in this setup picture (circled in red) is in a group being herded across the slope.  That’s Mt Margaret in the foreground with Spirit Lake and Mt St Helens to the right.  At the same time, I spotted a very vocal bull on a flat area at the bottom of the slope.  See the challenger (circled in red) with his big antlers, who now obviously wanted the cow’s attentions.  He started a deliberate walk to the moving herd.  Soon I could see the herd’s master (circled in red) coming into my view.  The distance he allowed between himself and the herd turned out to be his critical mistake.  He looked a little smaller than the big guy coming up the hill.  The master’s ladies were obediently moving along as shown here.  The red arrow points to the challenger, who has smartly ducked into the trees and to the left of the barren gully on his ascent.  It looked like the herd was going to cross the gully.  Indeed they crossed it and at just the right time, the challenger struts out of the trees, cut’s off the herd from the soon-to-be former master, and stares him down.  The former master now takes a non-threatening posture by turning away probably wondering what he did wrong.  He very slowly walks away always watching over his shoulder.  At this point, the new master has taken over.  He quickly gathers his new herd by collecting and herding the wanderers away from the former master and into a tightly controlled group.  So that’s the male side of things.  The females are only interested in munching on grass and once again obediently follow the new master’s orders.  I guess a cow doesn’t care who gives the orders as long as there’s grass to eat.  A little extra herding and the bull can relax and declare victory.  The herd’s new bull now triumphantly bugles his success.  The sex orgy hadn’t started by the time I left.

After it all, I wondered how the smaller bull got control of the herd with the bigger one so close by.  I suppose these herd coups happen pretty regularly.  It wasn’t mean or violent.  It happened with a few efficient and deliberate movements.  Maybe the next day the herd’s new master let his guard down and a new guy moved in with the same skill I saw that day.

Until next time, I’m John Speth, for our Wild America!
