Camp Muir on Mt Rainier, Washington - September 24, 2006

I finally decided that Mt Rainier National Park was too close to miss visiting it another year.  Mt Rainier National Park is a supremely beautiful place.  Mt Rainier is a HUGE mountain (top at 14,410 ft) with HUGE glaciers all around.  Climbing it is not what someone does casually or without the right equipment and skills.  I decided to visit Camp Muir on a good weather day and then return home.  It was nothing more than a long high altitude hike, half of it over snow.  Camp Muir at about 10,000 ft is the first and usually the only base camp at which most climbers will acclimate before their final push to the top.  It’s also an odd alpine urban settlement that seems so out of place.  Climbing outfitters make a decent pile of money guiding tourists from all over the world to the top so I’m sure they’re behind this out-of-place installation of shacks. 

The hike up was uneventful as far as events go.  The scenery was constantly stunning though.  I got a good early start at 5am with nobody else in sight.

Here’s Mt Rainier during the morning’s first golden light.

After four hours of trudging uphill I finally arrived at Camp Muir.  The camp is a mish-mash of some handsome and some shanty looking buildings.  All taken together, it looked out of place and unsightly.  I later learned that, before the National Park Service got the situation under control, it was much worse, sewerage-wise.  Now there are latrines and outfitter’s private bunking facilities.  The outfitters are expected to manage the human-related problems so I suppose the urbanized camp is necessary.

Here’s a group of skiers waiting for the snow to soften up before starting down.  They’re standing on the public bunkhouse doorway platform.

The back of the public bunkhouse with the park ranger “station” A-frame in the background:

Here’s the inside of the public bunkhouse.  There’s room for maybe up to 30 people to sleep depending on how close you want to get to you’re neighbor.  It’s two big plywood slabs held together with 2x4s.  It’s dry and not windy!

The latrines are the brown wooden buildings – very smelly and right outside the bunkhouse door.  The stone building in the background is a guide company’s kitchen.

Here’s the kitchen building with the public bunkhouse and latrines in the background.

Here’s the ranger station A-frame.  It’s tiny but wind free!  That’s Gibraltar Rock looming above and to the right.

More waiting. It was a pretty cold morning.

I climbed a little knob to get a better view of the camp’s surroundings.  Here’s a birds-eye view of Camp Muir.

Here’s the top of the Cowlitz Glacier – one of the smaller chunks of ice on the mountain but huge nonetheless.

Here’s a close-up peek of a crevasse at the top of the Cowlitz Glacier.  Crevasses are literally death traps.  If you don’t believe me watch the dramatic documentary “Touching the Void”.

Here’s Gibraltar Rock in all its glory wrapped in glaciers.  It tops out at about 12,500 ft.

Here’s proof I was there.  A nice non-English speaking Japanese tourist took the picture.  He insisted.

That picture shows the south face of the mountain with the Nisqually Glacier running from the top to well into the canyon that guides the ice to the Nisqually River headwaters.  Only half of the glacier’s white ice is visible.  The rest of it is covered in glacial debris.  It goes well beyond the view of the picture.

Later in November, the Nisqually River channeled a glacial outburst that resulted from several days of warm heavy rain on the mountain.  In a few hours it completely washed away the road beyond the southwest Nisqually park entrance including a campground and a bridge or two.
