Bats Feasting on Metolius River Bugs, Camp Sherman, Oregon – August 28, 2008

This one isn’t a hike.  Nick, Nancy, and I took our late summer vacation to the quiet and beautiful Camp Sherman, Oregon on the Metolius River near Sisters where we were treated to the nightly feast of bats gorging on the river bugs.  The Metolius River starts at the base of Black Butte near Camp Sherman from a concentration of high output springs.  It’s truly a small river and an instant river at its start.  Along its way to the Deschutes River, the Metolius builds from the contributions of numerous other springs that feed the stream to a fast running river that flows at nearly a constant rate all year round.  The Metolius is also a well known and productive fly fishing river due to the huge number of tasty bugs that hatch during the warm months.  It’s a cold, fast, and clean river that feeds the fish amply.  At night, the bats move in to take the last remaining bugs that swarm the river.

Right at about sunset, the bugs come out in huge numbers at what seems like an instant and swarm around the river and its banks.  Within a quarter hour the bats move in.  After a half hour, the bats are swarming as much as the bugs.  They move like quick shadows, silently and with great precision.  The night I took these pictures, I could feel several brush by my head.  I doubt I was ever contacted but I certainly felt the wind of their wings as they dodged me.

The folks at Canon were kind enough to replace our 7 year old recalled digital S230 camera with a refurbished SD850 camera at no charge (the best deal I’d gotten on anything for a long time!).  Learning to use the new camera and hoping for luck to capture a target that moves fast, can’t be seen in the dark, and on which focusing is nearly impossible was a real challenge.  I took about a hundred pictures and I got only two good ones and several other faint ones.

The bats are like little winged mice.  They had reddish brown fur and very black wings.  Their navigational skills are impressive.  It’s amazing an animal’s sonar can be so developed to locate and catch a bug in an erratic flight that is no bigger than a dime at the speeds that these bats fly!
