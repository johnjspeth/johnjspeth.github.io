Old Snowy – September 22, 2005 (and a revisit on August 12, 2006)

The Goat Rock Wilderness has a big reputation for rugged scenic beauty in the shadow of Mt Rainier.  And the Pacific Crest Trail (PCT) winds right through it.  It’s also highest point of the entire length of the PCT.  It’s a long drive from my home so my visit would have to fit my email in sick/sleep in the car/hike the next day protocol.  I set up my new computer program that would automatically email my call-in-sick email at 6:45am the next day and then I left home at 8pm.  I arrived for bed by 11pm.  I was up and fed by 4am to start my hike – destination: Old Snowy Mountain in Goat Rocks.

I started my hike under very bright half moon light.  The tree shadows were so dark that I still had to use my headlight.  Within 10 minutes of starting I noticed two brown disks glowing about 20 ft ahead on the trail.  I thought it might be an owl because I didn’t hear any noise.  I took a few steps and started to get concerned that these disks were way too far apart to be an owl.  Now I had a problem: Was I looking at some sort of trail marker or a very large animal?  I stood there a minute and made a loud grunt to get some sort of reaction from the unknown thing.  The grunt spooked a very large elk and it took off running.  Being chocolate brown and in the night shadows, he was invisible to me until he ran on.  He was big: his shoulders at my eye level.  He must have been under the spell of my headlight.  Onward.

Progress was good.  I made it Snowgrass Flats (next picture) well before sunrise.  There was a thin cloud cover that drew out and muted the sunrise.  Here’s my first look at my destination: Old Snowy is the left-most mountain on the ridge.  To the right is Ives Peak and then Mt Curtis-Gilbert.  The junction with the PCT is nearby ahead.

Hiking over Snowgrass Flats was a lot of fun.  There were lots of ups and downs with mini cliffs all around.  It was easy to see from the top of Old Snowy later that the area was carved out by glaciers not too long ago.  In every area there was either a glacial deposit or gouge.  Here’s a closer look at Old Snowy below.  The PCT climbs over the ridge that goes off the picture to the left.  My route is along the ridgeline from the PCT to the peak on the right.

Here’s my first view of Mt Rainier upon reaching the ridge crest at 7000 ft by following the PCT.  The sun still hadn’t touched me.  I think Mt Rainier is a very handsome mountain!

As soon as I got to Snowgrass Flats, I noticed a pair of hikers coming my way fast in the distance along the PCT.  They finally caught up with me at the ridge crest.  The conversation went something like this:

How ya doin?

Great!  Nice day for a hike, not too hot.

Where ya comin’ from?

I don’t know the name.  We camped somewhere down there.

Are you hiking the PCT?

Yep.

Where’d you start?

Mexico.

Wow!  How long ya been hiking?

Five months.

How much longer will you hike?

About two more weeks.

Well I hope you don’t get too much snow.  It’s late.

We already got snow.  Wasn’t pretty.  (All they had were sneakers)

Ok. Enjoy your trip and congratulations.  Oh, turn around and smile.  Thanks.

Here’s Goat Lake at 6000 ft - a giant hole that fills with snow melt at the end of Goat Ridge.

Here’s my last steep pitch to the peak of Old Snowy.  That’s Mt Adams in the distance.

From the peak of Old Snowy and along the ridge is Ives Peak on the right and Mt Curtis-Gilbert on the left.  The snow is various glaciers on the north side.

On the way down, I took a shortcut through a glacier deposition zone and came across some hiker whimsy: a mini Stonehenge.  Somebody must have spent a lot of time looking for just the right sized rectangular rocks.

 It was a long hike, about 14 miles.  Aside from the PCT backpackers I only saw two other backpackers.  I didn’t see the area’s namesake wildlife: mountain goats.  I’ve heard they are around there but obviously not today.

I got home and realized why my pictures were so grainy.  I must have fiddled with the ISO number for night time pictures weeks ago and never set it back.  I’m sorry the pictures couldn’t be as crisp as they should be. And that automatic sick email program I tested several times and started the day before didn’t work the one time I needed it to.  I had some ‘splainin to do the next day.

REVISIT:

I returned to this most beautiful place on August 12, 2006, very early on a Saturday.  I wanted to explore the Goat Lake area and look for the elusive goats.  I hiked to the top of Hawkes Peak overlooking Goat Lake (it’s the highest point on the cirque surrounding the lake in the picture above).  The Goat Ridge trail is a direct route to Goat Lake and much more scenic than the trail I took last year. 

When I finally arrived at the peak, I discovered the goats like to hang out near the ridge tops of Lilly Basin, a little visited glacial valley on “the other side” of Goat Lake.  The ridge to Hawkes Peak separates the two valleys and most people stay on the Goat Lake side.  I got to the top and planted myself for a long look for goats.  After some searching I caught a peek through the cracks of a crag of a trio of goats about a hundred feet from my perch.  They hadn’t seen me yet so I crept over to get a closer look.  At that point my profile was in their view.  They carefully and purposefully jogged away.  It wasn’t a trot across a flat meadow.  They scampered over steep slippery rock slopes and even over what looked like cliff from my vantage point above.  They finally joined a herd of 20 or so across the basin where they laid down and sunned themselves in safety.

The goat show ended and I headed down to see Goat Lake.  The lake was 50% filled with snow. The surrounding slopes were covered in wildflowers thriving from the constant feed of snow melt.  This might be where the Garden of Eden was located.

My hike out was fun but I constantly encountered the weekend hordes of campers.  The visitation season is short in Goats Rocks (July through September) and after that, the area is locked in snow.

I’m sorry I can’t show pictures.  My camera was on vacation on Pennsylvania.
