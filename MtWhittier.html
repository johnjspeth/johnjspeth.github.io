Mt Whittier, Washington – November 4, 2009

The 2009 autumn season began like any other, a lot of rain and a few significant snow dumps in the mountains.  I've had my eye on traversing the Mount Whittier Ridge near Mount Saint Helens for the past five years but the opportunity never arrived.  This season a couple of things fell into place which allowed me to take on my Mount Whittier mission.  My hard-core hiking sister Ann from Maine was visiting in late October.  She arrived during a weeks long period of cold and dry weather during which most new snow either melted or evaporated.  My Mount Whittier opportunity was finally a reality: little snow in November and an enthusiastic hiking companion.

Mount Whittier is a two-mile steep sided ridge line in the Mount Margaret backcountry near Mount Saint Helens.  The trail along this ridge line was built long before Mount Saint Helens erupted in 1980.  It appears to have been abandoned after the eruption.  The trail is officially closed but entirely hikable and somewhat treacherous in a few spots.  My disclaimer: The Mount Whittier trail should only be attempted in dry, low snow conditions by experienced hikers who are not subject to vertigo.

It's a three hour drive from my house to the trailhead at Norway Pass which meant I needed to pick up Ann at 3 AM, cutting short some necessary sleep.  This allowed us to make our 6:30 AM hiking start.  A minor annoyance on this hike is the 6 mile entry and exit penalty.  We chose to start the loop route so we could hike the ridge from the north to the south.  That took us along the Lakes Trail area (past many lakes) in the morning.  The lakes area was in the direct hit blast zone as you can see by all the flattened trees just about everywhere.

After crossing Bear Pass, we descended into the basins where the trail meandered from lake to lake.  With no trees to block our views, it was an especially scenic route.  We even saw an elk herd moving slowly through the downed trees. 

We passed about five lakes all of which have names, only one that I can recall.

Our first encounter with difficulty was the notorious trail washout not far from Pleasant Pass, which is consistently described in every hike synopsis on the web.  Believe it or not this next picture shows the “trail”.  We navigated from left to right.  The trail is a little more obvious in person but at some point in this traverse it really doesn't exist.  We found ourselves kicking toe-holes in the soft dirt, slowly moving hand-over-hand, small step by small step, and grabbing whatever we could with our hands for stability.  It's not the most comfortable way to hike when you trust your weight to be supported by hanging grass tufts and half exposed bush roots but in this case it was the only way to get through.  That 100 foot section took about a half hour to get through.

Here's a look backwards after crossing the slope, a fool's journey to say the least.  The snow patch in the foreground marks the end of the functioning trail.

And wouldn't you know it, when we got past the treacherous section and found the top of the ridge, we found what was probably a much safer route on the other side.  We'll never know for sure without a return visit.

At one point along the ridge the trail just seemed to end.  We wondered if it was a another washout.  A little careful scanning revealed that we needed to climb down about 200 feet and then across a wall along a narrow catwalk to return to the ridge top.  Somewhere in this picture you might be able to identify the catwalk (lower left).

Here's a closer view of the twin peaks of Mount Whittier.  Notice what appears to be a snow-covered trail in the bottom center.  That is actually an elk trail, one of many scattered all over.  The elks seem to have no problem traveling in this area.  Just beyond the peak is an obvious elk pass shared by mountain goats too.

Here’s Ann sitting on the top of Mount Whittier.  That is Mount Rainier in the background.  It was quite cold and windy up there at about 5800 feet.

It was Mission Accomplished and it was time for the seven mile walk back to the car.  I was particularly exhausted on the last four miles, cursing the uphills and savoring the downhills.
