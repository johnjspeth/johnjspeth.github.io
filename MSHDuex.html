Mt St Helens, Washington, Post 2004 Eruption – November 1, 2006

In September 2004 Mt St Helens started an eruption cycle that eventually resulted in a new crater dome.  There were mildly violent explosions and they quickly closed the area to hiking and climbing.  In 2005, they opened the area to hiking in the areas of the mountain not facing the crater.  In the summer of 2006 they finally opened Mt St Helens for climbing.  With the opening came more limiting rules and higher fees for climbers.  For a mere $22 you can sweat your way to the top from April to October only if you’re lucky enough to get your reservation in early enough (early by years not months).  The ultimate insult is served when, if you climb during the spring snow season, you find that those obnoxious snomobilers are at the top free-of-charge.  It just cost them the gas and a coupla quarts of 2-cycle oil to get there.

I’ve been to the top of Mt St Helens so many times I’ve lost count but I still love the climb.  This fall I made an appointment with myself to see the new dome.  The climb up was cold and very comfy with very few other climbers around.  It was nothing out of the ordinary.  It was pictures of the new dome and hopefully a few pictures of a steam explosion I was after.

This picture is the best I could do to get both domes in one shot.  The new dome is on the left and the old dome is on the right.  The old dome still steams a little but not nearly as much as the new dome.  That’s the north end of Sprit Lake in the top right.

Here’s the same vantage point to include Mt Rainier – so far away yet looking so big.

What makes the new dome so interesting is the way it grows.  The magma is under so much pressure while it’s forced up the chimney that it flows like putty.  As it reaches the air, it solidifies and ends up looking like a play-dough extrusion.  The extrusion grows vertically until it collapses under its own weight.  The geologists report that the process is constant and consistent all the time.  This picture shows the extrusion walls.  Also you can barely make out the heat ripples near the top of the dome.

Here’s a wider angle view of the new dome.  It’s very geometrically uniform.  It’s also pushing straight through a glacier long since melted over the past two years.  The glacier remains surrounded by the dome on the left.  Part of the old dome is on the right.

I got lucky and witnessed a tiny quiet steam release.  A puff of steam came out and drifted away.

In all my visits, I never made it to the official mountain peak because there wasn’t much to see in that direction.  That’s changed now with the new dome growing right below the peak.  The peak is marked by a pile of rocks in this picture.

The day’s hike finally satisfied a bug in my brain to see the new activity since the 2004 eruption.  I’m glad I did it.
